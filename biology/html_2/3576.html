Isolation, Purification and Characterization of Exosomes from Fibroblast Cultures of Skeletal Muscle

Authors: Diantha van de Vlekkert, Xiaohui Qiu, Ida Annunziata, Alessandra d'Azzo

Abstract

Exosomes are dynamic nanovesicles secreted by virtually all cells and are present in all biological fluids. Given their highly heterogeneous content exosomes have been implicated in many physiological and pathological processes that they exert by influencing cell-cell and cell-ECM communication. In recent years an increasing number of methods have been established for the purification and characterization of exosomes. These include ultracentrifugation, ultrafiltration, size exclusion chromatography, immune capture and precipitation using a proprietary polymer. Here, we provide a protocol based on differential ultracentrifugation and sucrose density gradients tailored for the isolation of crude and ultra-pure exosomes from primary fibroblast cultures derived from adult mouse skeletal muscle. This protocol can be adapted and modified for the isolation and characterization of exosomes from a variety of tissues and bodily fluids.

Keywords: Fibroblasts, Cell culture, Exosomes, Ultracentrifugation, Sucrose gradient, Western blot

Background

Exosomes are single-membrane, heterogeneous nanovesicles ranging in diameter from 30 to 150 nm, secreted by all cells and present in virtually all body fluids. The spectrum of soluble and membrane macromolecules, mRNAs, microRNAs present in exosomes depends on the metabolic status as well as the developmental origin of the cell(s) secreting these nanovesicles. Owing to their cargo composition, exosomes can initiate signaling pathways in the receiving cells and have been involved in development, immunity and in the maintenance of normal tissue physiology. In disease conditions, like neurodegeneration, fibrosis and cancer, they have been shown to trigger and propagate pathological stimuli (Rackov et al., 2018; Gurunathan et al., 2019; van de Vlekkert et al., 2019). Here we describe a protocol for the purification of exosomes from fibroblast cultures established from adult mouse gastrocnemius (GA) muscle (van de Vlekkert et al., 2019). These purified exosomal preparations can be subjected to mass spectrometry. The advantage of this method is that is scalable and cost effective. Primary mouse fibroblasts are kept in culture for no more than 4 passages to preserve their primary status, and their conditioned medium is then processed by differential centrifugation to obtain crude exosomal preparations that are further purified by sucrose density gradients. This protocol has been successfully used to assess and compare the protein composition of exosomes released by normal mouse fibroblasts and by activated fibroblasts (myofibroblasts) cultured from the GA muscle of WT and Neu1â/â mice, respectively (van de Vlekkert et al., 2019). The Neu1â/â mouse is a model of the lysosomal storage disease sialidosis, caused by deficiency of the sialidase neuraminidase 1 (NEU1) (de Geest et al., 2002; dâAzzo et al., 2015). Mouse cells deficient for Neu1, including primary myofibroblasts, have excessive exocytosis of soluble lysosomal contents as well as increased release of exosomes (van de Vlekkert et al., 2019). Exosomes purified from Neu1â/â myofibroblasts were shown to be potent carriers of fibrotic signaling molecules and to be responsible for eliciting a pathogenic cascade, which ultimately led to muscle fibrosis and degeneration (van de Vlekkert et al., 2019).

### Procedure

  1. Muscle excision
    1. Euthanize the mouse following guidelines approved by the institution.
      Notes:
      1. Both male and female mice can be used.
      2. For this protocol a genetically engineered animal model of the lysosomal storage disease sialidosis (de Geest et al., 2002) crossed into C57Bl/6 or FVB background was used.
    2. The isolation of the hind limb muscles does not require the use of a biosafety cabinet. No sterile conditions are necessary.
    3. Pin the mouse down in a prone position (dorsal side up) to the dissecting pad (Figure 1A). Spray the mouse with 70% ethanol.
      Note: For the isolation of other muscle types, pin the mouse down in a supine position (ventral side up) to the dissecting board.
    4. Remove the skin from both hind limbs to expose the muscles and gently cut away the fascia of both hind limb muscles. Locate the Achilles tendon and separate it from the bone with a pair of forceps (Figure 1B).
      Notes:
      1. Shaving the fur is not necessary for this procedure.
      2. There is not a standard method for the removal of the fur/skin to expose the muscles. The procedure we use is as follows: Make a ~1 cm cut in the skin at the level of the hip joint and carefully pull the skin towards the ankle to expose the leg muscles.
    5. Cut the Achilles tendon and carefully pull the GA muscle upwards towards the knee joint and cut it loose at the tendon (Figures 1C and 1D).
      Note: As the tendons from the GA and soleus are interconnected (forming the Achilles tendon) it is not possible to separate them.
    6. Remove the soleus (darker red colored part of the muscle). When the GA and soleus muscles are cut at the knee joint, the soleus will detach from the GA muscle and can be lifted up and cut at the Achilles tendon to separate it from the GA muscle (Figures 1E and 1F).
      Note: The removal of the soleus allows for a purer fibroblast isolation from the remaining GA muscle.
    7. Weight a 50 ml tube containing 5 ml DPBS.
    8. Combine the GA muscles from both legs and transfer them to the pre-weighted 50 ml tube and weight the tube again. Place the tube on ice.

The fur/skin is removed to expose the GA muscle (dashed lines) and the Achilles tendon, which is separated from the bone by a pair of forceps. C. The Achilles tendon is cut. D. The GA/soleus muscles are pulled upwards and cut at the knee joint. E. Isolated GA muscle is shown with the soleus (darker red and dashed lines) that is still attached. F. GA muscle is shown after removal of the soleus.

  2. Fibroblasts isolation from the gastrocnemius muscle
    1. Prepare the biosafety hood by placing all necessary tools inside, including tube racks, pipets, dishes, tubes, tip boxes.
    2. Take two scalpels out of the sterile wrap and place them in the hood.
    3. Place a 10 cm dish open in the hood.
    4. Take out the sterile forceps and transfer the two GA muscles into the 10 cm dish.
      Note: The tissues contain residual amount of DPBS, which keeps the tissue moist during the procedure.
    5. Chop the muscles with the scalpels until it become a fine paste.
    6. Transfer the finely chopped muscle paste onto a 50 ml tube using the scalpels, cap the tube and place it back on ice.
      Notes:
      1. Additional DPBS is not required to transfer the minced muscle tissue with scalpels.
      2. If additional muscle samples need to be processed, repeat steps B4-B6 and transfer each chopped muscle paste to a new 50 ml tube.
    7. Add digestion mixture to each tube; 3.5x volume/mg tissue (see Recipes 1-4) and incubate at 37 Â°C for 45 min.
      Note: Alternatively, a tissue dissociator can be used to dissociate the muscles.
    8. During the 45 min digestion period, the tissue suspension needs to be mixed every 10 min by pipetting it up and down with a 5 ml pipet. This will promote the complete dissociation of the tissue.
    9. Add 20 ml complete medium to each tube to terminate the enzymatic digestion (see Recipe 5).
    10. Place a 70 Î¼m cell strainer on a new 50 ml tube.
    11. Filter the tissue/cell suspension through the strainer, rinsing it with an additional 5.0 ml complete medium.
    12. Centrifuge the tissue/cell suspension at 300 x g at 4 Â°C for 10 min.
    13. Resuspend the pellet containing the cells in 10 ml complete medium.
    14. Seed the resuspended pellet in a 10 cm dish (Passage 0, P0) and incubate at 37 Â°C/5% CO2/3% O2.
      Note: The fibroblasts are cultured at a low oxygen level to keep the culture conditions as close as possible to the physiologic conditions, since the O2 level in skeletal muscle is around 2.5% (Mas-Bargues et al., 2019).

  3. Fibroblasts culture, trypsinization and expansion
    1. Culture the P0 fibroblasts until 100% confluency plus 1 day.
      Notes:
      1. Primary fibroblasts at P0 are routinely cultured until 100% confluency plus 1 day (and only for P0) to purge the cultures from any contaminating cells.
      2. The expected yield of fibroblasts (P0) from one pair of hind limb gastrocnemius muscles ranges from 1 to 5 x 106 cells, depending on the age and background of the mouse used.
    2. When cells are ready to be expanded, remove the culture medium and rinse the cells with 10 ml DPBS.
      Note: Be sure to remove all DPBS because it may interfere with the TrypLE express enzymatic activity.
    3. Add 1.0 ml TrypLE to the dish and incubate at 37 Â°C for 5 min.
    4. Tap the side of the dish and check under the microscope for complete detachment of the cells. If necessary, incubate another 5 min at 37 Â°C.
    5. Add 10 ml complete medium to inactivate the TrypLE and transfer the cells into a 50 ml tube.
    6. Centrifuge at 300 x g for 10 min at room temperature.
      Note: At this point P0 cells can be resuspended in freezing medium to be frozen for later use (see Recipe 6).
    7. Resuspend the cells in 10 ml complete medium.
    8. If needed, cells can be counted by mixing 10 Î¼l cell suspension with 10 Î¼l Trypan Blue and transferred to the Countess cell counter chamber.
      Note: Any type of cell counter can be used as long as it gives an accurate count of viable cells.
    9. Cells are seeded at a concentration of 1.0 x 105/ml, resulting in 2 x 106 cells (20 ml) per 15 cm dish.
    10. Culture the cells at 37 Â°C/5% CO2/3% O2 until 80%-90% confluency (Passage 1, P1).
    11. Repeat Steps C2 to C10 for additional passaging of the culture.
      Notes:
      1. The number of 15 cm dishes needed will depend on the total number of cells required; e.g., at P2 cells are seeded in 2-4, 15 cm dishes; at P4 they are seeded in 15-30, 15 cm dishes if a high yield of exosomes is required.
      2. The yield of exosomes from a 15 cm dish of primary fibroblasts is ~3-4 Î¼g. We suggest seeding 20-25, 15 cm dishes to obtain enough exosomes for subsequent analyses.
      3. After P4, primary cells are discarded and not used for any further experiments as they change their characteristics.

  4. Crude Exosome isolation
    1. Wash the 15 cm dishes containing fibroblasts (P2-P4) with 10 ml DPBS.
      Note: Be sure to remove all DPBS because it may interfere with the TrypLE express enzymatic activity.
    2. Add 2.5 ml TrypLE to each dish and incubate at 37 Â°C for 5 min.
    3. Tap the side of the dish and check under the microscope for complete detachment of the cells. If necessary, incubate another 5 min at 37 Â°C.
    4. Add 10 ml complete medium to inactivate the TrypLE and transfer the cells to a 50 ml tube.
    5. Centrifuge the cells at 300 x g for 10 min at room temperature.
    6. Resuspend the cells in 10 ml complete exosome free medium (see Recipes 7 and 8).
      Note: All vesicles/exosomes naturally present in FBS need to be removed prior to adding the FBS to the fibroblast culture medium, as they will interfere with further experimental analyses.
    7. If needed, cells can be counted by mixing 10 Î¼l cell suspension with 10 Î¼l Trypan Blue and transferred to the Countess cell counter chamber.
      Note: Any type of cell counter can be used as long as it gives an accurate count of viable cells.
    8. Seed the cells in the exosome free medium at the concentration of 1.5 x 106 - 2.0 x 106 per 15 cm dish and incubate at 37 Â°C/5% CO2/3% O2.
    9. Collect the conditioned medium in 50 ml tubes on ice after 16 to 24 h.
      Notes:
      1. The collected medium can be stored at 4 Â°C up to one week, if not processed immediately.
      2. If cells are not 80% confluent at the time of medium harvesting, fresh exosomal medium can be added and collected after an additional 16 to 24 h period. Combine the two batches of conditioned medium for further processing.
    10. Centrifuge the conditioned medium at 300 x g for 10 min at 4 Â°C and transfer it to a new 50 ml tube on ice (Figure 2.1).
      Note: This step is performed to eliminate viable cells.
    11. Centrifuge at 2,000 x g for 10 min at 4 Â°C (Figure 2.2).
      Note: This step is performed to eliminate dead cells.
    12. Transfer supernatant to a thin wall Polypropylene tube on ice and centrifuge in an ultracentrifuge at 10,000 x g at 4 Â°C for 30 min (Figure 2.3).
      Note: This step removes organelles, apoptotic bodies and membrane fragments. Centrifugation at 10,000 x g can also be performed using a Sorval centrifuge.
    13. Transfer the supernatant to an ultra-clear tube on ice and ultracentrifuge it at 100,000 x g at 4 Â°C for 1.5 h (Figure 2.4).
      Note: If exosomes need to be under sterile condition, the open ultra-clear tubes can be exchanged for sterile ultra-clear tubes with lids.
    14. Carefully discard the supernatant with a 10 ml pipette. Leave about 1.0 ml of liquid in the tube to avoid disturbance of the pellet.
    15. Add an additional 1.0 ml of ice-cold DPBS and resuspend the pellet using a P1000 pipette.
    16. Fill the tube up to 35 ml with ice-cold DPBS and ultracentrifuge at 100,000 x g at 4 Â°C for 1.5 h (Figure 2.5).
    17. Carefully discard the supernatant and leave the tube containing the pellet on ice.
      Note: The pellet should be translucent and may be hard to visualize, therefore the supernatant should be removed very carefully.
    18. Resuspend the pellet in an appropriate volume of ice-cold DPBS (~25 Î¼l/15 cm dish).
    19. The exosomes can be kept at 4 Â°C up to one week. For long-term storage exosomes should be stored at -80 Â°C.
      Notes:
      1. Avoid freezing/thawing cycles, which can damage exosomes and reduce their numbers. The exosomes should be aliquoted if multiple experiments are performed.
      2. The crude exosomes can be used for a variety of analyses, including Western blots and in vitro uptake experiments.
      3. When needed, mass spec analysis can be performed on crude exosomes. Run fifty Î¼g of crude exosomes on a 10% bis-tris gel for ~1 cm into the gel and cut 3 equal sized bands for in-gel digestion (sequencing grade modified trypsin solution) prior to LC-MS/MS.

    3. Assemble the precast gel in the electrophoresis tank and fill the tank with running buffer.
      Notes:
      1. See instruction manual from Bio-Rad (https://www.bio-rad.com/) for âMini-PROTEAN Tetra Cell Precastâ.

    19. Rinse the membrane 1 time with TBS-T and wash 3 times 10 min with TBS-T under gentle agitation.
    20. Pipet 1.0 ml of SuperSignal west Femto developer solution on a plexiglass plate, making sure that the table/bench is leveled to avoid run off of the solution (Recipe 26).
    21. Remove excess of TBS-T and place the membrane face down on 1.0 ml developer solution.
