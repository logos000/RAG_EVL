Evaluation of Mucosal and Systemic Vaccine Responses by Cyclic di-GMP (CDG)-adjuvanted Protein Subunit Vaccines

Authors: Samira Mansouri, Lei Jin

Abstract

Intranasal administration of vaccine adjuvants directly deliver therapeutic agents to the lungs to induce potent lung mucosal immune responses. Cyclic di-GMP (CDG) is a promising mucosal vaccine adjuvant candidate capable of inducing protective immunity. This protocol describes an in vivo approach to induce and detect mucosal (lung) and systemic (blood and spleen) vaccine adjuvant responses of CDG. This protocol also includes the methods to detect both humoral and cellular immune responses of CDG adjuvant. Last, this protocol can be used to study other cyclic dinucleotides as mucosal vaccine adjuvants.

Keywords: Cyclic dinucleotides, Mucosal vaccine adjuvant, Intranasal immunization, Humoral and cellular vaccine responses, Ex vivo recall assay

Background

Cyclic diguanylate monophosphate (CDG) is part of a new class of vaccine adjuvants called cyclic dinucleotides currently being used in clinical trials for the treatment of cancer and infectious diseases. In vitro and in vivo administration of CDG can induce strong immune responses by engaging the STING pathway to induce production of type I interferon and TNFÎ± (McWhirter et al., 2009; Blaauboer et al., 2014). As a potential mucosal vaccine adjuvant, CDG is well known to induce protective immunity against bacterial pathogens by producing strong antibody and Th1/Th2/Th17 responses in the lung (Ebensen et al., 2007; Yan et al., 2009; Madhun et al., 2011). Mechanistically, this mucosal adjuvant acts through the lung resident dendritic cells (Blaauboer et al., 2014; Mansouri et al., 2019). To date, the cellular and molecular mechanisms of CDG have been mainly characterized by In vitro experiments with murine and human cultured cells. In vitro studies often use transfection reagents to administer CDG into cells. As CDG has two phosphate groups preventing it from directly passing through the cell membrane, the method of transfection may be artificial. Interestingly, CDG was found to be taken up by cells via pinocytosis in vivo (Blaauboer et al., 2015). This protocol describes a more physiological relevant approach to studying the adjuvant effect of CDG in vivo through intranasal immunization.

### Procedure

  1. Vaccine preparation
    1. Preparation of CDG
      1. Prepare sterile CDG stock (2 mg/ml) with endotoxin-free physiological water in a 1.5 ml sterile Eppendorf tube. 
      2. Mix the solution by pipetting up and down.
      3. Store at -80 Â°C. The CDG stock retains its activity for at least 3 years in our lab.
    2. Preparation of antigens
      1. Prepare sterile stock antigen (PspA, OVA or H1N1 NP) solution (1 mg/ml) with endotoxin-free PBS.
      2. Mix the solution by pipetting up and down.
      3. Store at -80 Â°C. The protein antigen stocks are stable for at least 3 years in our lab.

  2. Intranasal vaccine administration
    1. At Day 0, freshly prepare a suspension containing 5 Âµg of CDG and 2 Âµg of antigen per mouse in endotoxin-free 1x PBS for experimental samples.
    2. At Day 0, freshly prepare a suspension containing 2 Âµg of antigen per mouse in endotoxin-free PBS for control samples.
    3. Anesthetize the mouse by placing in the isoflurane chamber.
      1. To use the machine, turn on the supply gas and have the flowmeter between 500-1,000 ml/min.
      2. Turn on vaporizer to 5%.
      3. Place the mouse in the chamber and seal the top.
      4. Wait until the mouse is anesthetized.
    4. Remove the mouse from the chamber and hold the mouse in your hand by the dorsal skin so that the head is up.
    5. Administer 30 Âµl of the vaccine solution into animalâs nostril. Let the animal breathes in the vaccine solution. 
    6. Wait for 14 days before administrating a second immunization (Total 2 immunizations).

  3. Collection of sera to measure antigen-specific antibodies in the blood
    1. CDG-induced serum antibodies can be detected at 2-week, 1-month, 3-month, and 6-month after the last immunization.
    2. Collect blood from the tail of each mouse.
      1. Cut as close to the bottom of the mouse tail.
      2. Collect around 50-100 Âµl of blood and place in a serum collection tube.
      3. Spin samples down at 600 x g at RT for 10 min.
      4. Collect the supernatant and store samples at -20 Â°C.

  4. Harvest of bronchoalveolar fluid (BALF) to measure antigen-specific antibodies in the lung
    1. CDG-induced BALF antibodies can be detected at 2-week, 1-month, 3-month, and 6-month after the last immunization.
    2. Sacrifice mouse with CO2. 
    3. Clean the mouse with 70% ethanol.
    4. Place the mouse on a styrofoam board and restrain the arms and legs with needles.
    5. Remove the skin around the neck to expose the thoracic cage and neck.
    6. Gently remove the muscles around the neck with forceps to expose the trachea.
    7. Using scissors, make a small cut on the surface of the trachea. 
    8. Load a 1 ml syringe with 0.8 ml of 1x PBS.
    9. Attach a BD Instyle Autoguard BC Shielded IV Catheter to the 0.8 ml syringe and insert into the trachea.
    10. Slowly inject and recover the 1x PBS into a 1.5 ml Eppendorf tube, and place on ice.
    11. Spin down samples at 350 x g at 4 Â°C for 5 min. 
    12. Transfer the supernatant to a new 1.5 ml Eppendorf tube and place on ice.
    13. Store samples at -20 Â°C.

  5. Detection of antigen-specific IgG, IgG1, IgG2C (C57BL/6 mice) or IgG2A (BALB/c mice) and IgA production in the serum and BALF
    1. Coat an ELISA assay plate using 1 Âµg/ml of antigen (PspA, H1N1 NP or OVA) in coating buffer.
      1. Add 50 Âµl per well.
      2. Keep plate at 4 Â°C overnight.
    2. Wash the plate 5 times using wash buffer. 
    3. Add 100 Âµl of blocking buffer to each well and leave at room temperature for 1 h.
    4. After blocking is done, wash the plate 5 times with wash buffer.
    5. Dilute sera samples starting with 1:5,000 in blocking buffer at 2-fold dilutions for 4 dilutions.
    6. Dilute the BALF samples starting with 1:100 in blocking buffer at 2-fold dilutions for 4 dilutions.
    7. Add 50 Âµl of each serial dilution to the respective wells.
    8. Keep plate at 4 Â°C overnight.
    9. Wash the plate 5 times using wash buffer.
    10. Add secondary antibodies conjugated to HRP to each well and leave at room temperature for 2 h.
    11. Wash the plate 5 times using wash buffer.
    12. Add 50 Âµl TMB solution to each well and incubate for 15 min.
    13. Add 50 Âµl stop solution.
    14. Read samples at 450 nm on a plate reader.

  6. Recall assay to measure antigen-specific T cell responses in the lung (mucosal responses, carry out under sterile conditions)
    1. Harvest the lung at 2-week, 1-month, 3-month, and 6-month after the last immunization.
      1. To isolate lung cells, sacrifice immunized mice with CO2.
      2. Clean the mouse with 70% ethanol.
      3. Open the rib cage to expose the heart and lungs.
      4. Cut the aorta above the liver.
      5. Using sterile 1x PBS, inject 10 ml of cold 1x PBS into the left ventricle to perfuse the lungs. Perfused lung should be white.
      6. Transfer the lungs into 1.5 ml Eppendorf tubes that contain 500 Âµl of ice-cold 1x PBS to wash the lungs.
      7. Add the lungs to 1.5 ml Eppendorf tubes that contain 1 ml digestion buffer and keep at 37 Â°C for 1.5 h. The digesting buffer has been prewarmed to 37 Â°C.
    2. Make a single cell suspension after digestion
      1. Use a 1 ml syringe plunger to press the digested lung tissue through a 70 Âµm mesh with sterile RMPI and into a sterile 50 ml tube.
      2. Spin the samples down at 350 x g for 5 min at 4 Â°C.
      3. Decant the supernatant. 
      4. Add 1 ml of ice-cold sterile ACK lysis buffer to the pellet.
        1. Disaggregate the cell pellet, re-suspend and transfer the cells to a 1.5 ml Eppendorf tube and invert for 2 min at RT.
        2. Spin tubes down at 350 x g for 5 min at 4 Â°C. 
        3. Decant supernatant.
      5. Disaggregate the cell pellet and re-suspend cells in 1 ml of sterile RPMI and count cells. Lung harvested from one 8~16 weeks old WT C57BL/6 mouse typically yield ~75 million total cells with a cell viability > 90%. 
      6. Spin samples down at 350 x g for 5 min.
      7. Re-suspend in 10% Culture Media to a concentration of 50-60 million cells/ml.
    3. Set up the ex vivo recall assay.
      1. In a 96-well round bottom tissue culture plate, 200 Âµl of lung cells are seeded.
      2. Add 1 Âµg of antigen into each well. The final antigen concentration is 5 Âµg/ml.
      3. Cells are cultured at 37 Â°C and 5% CO2 for 4 days.
    4. Collect all supernatant to measure IFNÎ³, IL-5/IL-13, IL-17A cytokine production using ELISA kits from eBioscienceTM.
      1. Transfer samples from each well to Eppendorf tubes and spin down at 350 x g for 5 min.
      2. Transfer supernatant to new Eppendorf tubes.
      3. Store at -20 Â°C.

  7. Recall assay to measure antigen-specific T cell responses in the spleen (systemic responses, carry out under sterile conditions)
    1. Make a single cell suspension
      1. Harvest the spleen at 2-week, 1-month, 3-month, and 6-month after the last immunization.
      2. Use a 1ml syringe plunger to press the smashed spleen tissue through a 70 Âµm mesh with sterile ice-cold RPMI with 2% FCS/1 mM EDTA into a sterile 50 ml tube.
      3. Spin the samples down at 350 x g for 5 min at 4 Â°C.
      4. Carefully aspirated the supernatant.
      5. Add ice-cold sterile ACK lysis buffer (5 ml) to the pellet.
        1. Disaggregate the cell pellet, re-suspend cells and left at RT for 5 min.
        2. Add 20 ml ice-cold RPMI/2% FCS/1 mM EDTA to spin tubes down at 350 x g for 5 min at 4 Â°C. 
        3. Decant supernatant.
      6. Add 1 ml of sterile ice-cold RPMI/2% FCS/1 mM EDTA.
      7. Disaggregate the cell pellet and resuspend cells.
      8. Transfer the cells to 1.5 ml Eppendorf tubes and count cells.
      9. Spin samples down at 350 x g for 5 min 4 Â°C.
      10. Re-suspend in 10% Culture Media to a concentration of 50-60 million cells/ml.
    2. Set up the ex-vivo activation system
      1. In a 96-well round bottom tissue culture plate, 200 Âµl of spleen cells are seeded.
      2. Add 1 Âµg of antigen into each well. The final antigen concentration is 5 Âµg/ml.
      3. Cells are cultured at 37 Â°C and 5% CO2 for 4 days.
    3. Collect all supernatant to measure IFNÎ³, IL-5/IL-13, IL-17A cytokine production using ELISA kits from eBioscienceTM.
