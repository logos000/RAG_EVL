Spectrophotometric Assessment of Heme Oxygenase-1 Activity in Leishmania-infected Macrophages

Authors: Moumita Basu, Shriya Saha, Anindita Ukil

Abstract

Heme oxygenase-1 (HO-1) is a stress responsive enzyme that metabolizes heme and releases free iron, carbon monoxide (CO), and biliverdin (BV), which rapidly undergoes conversion to bilirubin (BL). Estimation of bilirubin is the basis of HO-1 assay. HO-1 activity is widely employed to determine antioxidant response of cells under different physiological stress environment. Intra-macrophage infection often acts as such a stress inducer and measurement of HO-1 activity in infected cells indicates the ability of pathogens towards modulating oxidative response of host. The present protocol describes analysis of HO-1 activity in infected macrophages by spectrophotometric method, which is much less complex and therefore advantageous over other methods like high-performance liquid chromatography, radiochemical methods and detection of CO by gas chromatography. The main steps include: (1) Preparation of macrophage microsomal fraction containing HO-1 (2) Isolation of rat liver cytosolic fraction containing biliverdin reductase and (3) Assessment of heme oxygenase-1 activity by spectrophotometric detection of bilirubin. This method provides a simple and sensitive approach to measure cellular antioxidant response under infected condition.

Keywords: Heme oxygenase-1, Macrophage, Leishmania, Spectrophotometry, Bilirubin, Hemin

Background

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) is one of the major host defense arsenals against invading pathogens used by macrophages (Missall et al., 2004). On the other hand, intra-macrophage pathogens neutralize early oxidative burst for their successful persistence within macrophages (Paiva and Bozza, 2014). In response to such oxidative stress, organisms can deploy antioxidant enzymes of host cells such as superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT) glutathione peroxidase (GPX), and heme oxygenase-1 (HO-1) to scavenge ROS (Kathirvel et al., 2010). Intracellular parasite Leishmania donovani could effectively exploit host antioxidant enzyme HO-1 for ROS neutralization (Saha et al., 2019). HO-1 is a potent anti-oxidant enzyme catalysing the oxidative cleavage of heme to generate carbon monoxide (CO), ferrous iron (Fe2+), and biliverdin (BV). The biliverdin is further acted upon by another enzyme biliverdin reductase (BVR) to produce bilirubin (BL) (Tenhunen et al., 1969).There are several techniques to quantify the activity of HO-1 based on detection of one of its ultimate reaction product bilirubin via high-performance liquid chromatography (Lincoln et al., 1988; Ryter et al., 1999), visible spectrophotometry (Schacter, 1978; Tenhunen et al., 1969) and radiochemical methods (Sierra and Nutter, 1992). Detection of CO by gas chromatography (Vreman and Stevenson, 1988) has also been used to assay HO-1 activity, but because of its complexity and subsequent product analysis steps the protocol is not variedly applicable. Now, biliverdin is the primary metabolite of the heme degradation by HO-1. However, it has poor spectral properties with an extinction coefficient (Îµ) of ~8 to 10 mM-1 cm-1 (Kutty and Maines, 1981). Thus, most common HO-1 activity assays rely on the reduction of biliverdin to bilirubin. Original spectrophotometric quantification of bilirubin for detection of HO-1 activity was outlined by Tenhunen et al. (1969). In the method, bilirubin formation was monitored spectrophotometrically by the increase in absorbance at 468 nm (Îµ468 = 43.5 mM-1 cm-1), which is approximately 5-fold higher than that of biliverdin. Modifications of this main spectrophotometric assay for assessment of HO-1 activity was carried out and HO-1 activity was determined by monitoring bilirubin formation using the difference in absorbance at 464 to 530 nm (Îµ464-530 = 40 mM-1 cm-1) (Maines, 1996; Maines and Kappas, 1974).The current protocol (Figure 1) utilises the same principle but are performed with certain minor modifications to make it much more convenient.

### Procedure

  1. Preparation of microsomal fraction containing HO-1 enzyme
    1. Infect 80% confluent macrophage cells (RAW 264.7 or BMDM) in T25 flasks with Leishmania donovani promastigotes with a parasite to macrophage ratio (10:1) for the indicated time points (0, 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2 and 4 h).
    2. After incubation,discard the media and carefully wash the cells with 1 ml PBS solution and the steps were carried out by placing all the flasks on ice throughout.
    3. Add 1 ml potassium phosphate buffer to each of the flasks and gently scrape the cells using scrapers.
    4. Transfer the scraped cells in a 50 ml Falcon pre-chilled by placing on ice.
    5. Add 2 ml more potassium phosphate buffer.
    6. Incubate the cells for 15 min on ice.
    7. Briefly sonicate the cell suspensions at 80% amplitude for 15 s at 5 min interval for 3 times keeping on ice.
    8. Add sucrose solution to the cell lysate to obtain the final concentration 0.25 M sucrose.
    9. Centrifuge the solutions at 1,000 x g for 10 min at 4 Â°C.
      Note: The pellet will contain the nuclei.
    10. Centrifuge the supernatant at 12,000 x g for 15 min at 4 Â°C.
      Note: The pellet will contain the mitochondria.
    11. Ultracentrifuge the supernatant at 105,000 x g for 1 h at 4 Â°C.
    12. Discard the supernatant.
      Note:The pellet is the microsomal fraction containing HO-1 enzyme.
    13. Resuspend the pellet in 500 Âµl of 0.1 M potassium phosphate buffer.
    14. Store at -20 Â°C until use.

  2. Estimation of protein concentration (Bradford, 1976)
    1. Prepare 6 dilutions of bovine serum albumin (BSA) (10 mg/ml) as standard with a range of 0 to 50 Âµg protein in 995 Âµl diluted Bradford assay reagent (1:5) in duplicate and adding 5 Âµl from each of the test samples in 995 Âµl diluted Bradford assay reagent (1:5) in triplicate.
    2. Mix all the samples by gently inverting 2 to 3 times.
    3. Incubate all at room temperature for 5 min and read absorbance at 595 nm.
      Note: Do not keep the prepared solutions for more than 1 h at room temperature.

  3. Isolation of rat liver cytosolic fraction containing biliverdin reductase
    1. Euthanize a Sprague Dawley rat.
    2. Perfuse the liver with 0.9% NaCl solution in situ via hepatic portal vein until fully blanched.
    3. Excise out the blanched liver and mince it completely with help of scissors.
    4. Homogenize the liver sample in 5 ml of 0.1 M sodium citrate buffer using tissuelyzer (Bio-Rad)
    5. Centrifuge the homogenate at 10,000 x g for 20 min at 4 Â°C.
    6. Carefully pipette out the supernatant and ultracentrifuge the sup at 105,000 x g for 1 h at 4 Â°C.
    7. The supernatant serves as the source of biliverdin reductase.
    8. A portion of the obtained supernatant was tested for biliverdin reductase activity by assessing the amount of bilirubin produced from biliverdin via measuring the absorbance at 464 nm (bilirubin) and 670 nm (biliverdin). Five Âµg of protein from the obtained supernatant was incubated with 10 ÂµM biliverdin (Sigma) and 400 Âµg/ml bovine serum albumin (BSA) in 100 mM potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) at 37Â°C for 5 min in a total volume of 200 Âµl. The reaction was initiated by addition of 1 mM NADPH. The protein concentration of the supernatant was around 10 mg/ml.
    9. Store at -80 Â°C until use.
      Note:Use the solution as early as possible as the enzymes lose their activity upon storage.

  4. Assessment of heme oxygenase-1 activity
    1. Incubate 600 Âµg of microsomal protein with a reaction mixture containing 1 mM NAD(P)H, 2 mM glucose-6-phosphate, 1 U glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (Sigma-Aldrich), 25 ÂµM hemin, 2 mg of rat liver cytosolic protein, and 100 mM potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.4).
    2. Adjust the final volume to 400 Âµl with potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.4).
    3. Place the tubes in the dark for 1 h at 37 Â°C.
    4. After 1h, terminate the reaction by placing the tubes on ice for 2 min.
    5. Determine HO-1 activity by measuring bilirubin concentration by spectrophotometer.

  5. Spectrophotometric detection of bilirubin
  1. Determine bilirubin concentration by the difference in absorption between 464 and 530 nm (extinction coefficient, 40 mM-1 cm-1 for BL).
  2. Express HO-1 activity in picomoles of BL formed per milligram microsomal protein per hour (Figure 2).

### Recipes

  1. Phosphate buffer saline/buffer (pH 7.4)
    1. Add 8 g of NaCl (M.W. = 58.44 g/mol) in a sterilized 1 L bottle
    2. Add 1.44 g of Na2HPO4 (M.W. = 141.96 g/mol)
    3. Add 0.2 g of KCl (M.W. = 74.55 g/mol)
    4. Add 0.24 g of KH2PO4 (M.W. = 136.08 g/mol)
    5. Dissolve the reagents completely in about 800 ml sterilized distilled water (dH2O)
    6. Adjust the pH to 7.4 using 1 M HCl or 1 M NaOH (as required)
    7. Make up the final volume to 1 L with sterilized distilled water
    8. Sterilize the solution by autoclaving at 15 lbs pressure (121 Â°C) for 15 min
    9. Store the solution at 4 Â°C
  2. 0.1 M potassium phosphate buffer containing 2 mM MgCl2 and complete protease inhibitor (pH 7.4)
    1. Add 2.61 g of KH2PO4 (M.W. = 136.08 g/mol) in a sterilized 250 ml bottle
    2. Add 2.04 g of K2HPO4 (M.W. = 174.18 g/mol)
    3. Add 28.56 mg of MgCl2 (M.W. = 95.211 g/mol)
    4. Dissolve the reagents completely in about 120 ml sterilized distilled water (dH2O)
    5. Adjust the pH to 7.4 using 1 M HCl or 1 M NaOH (as required)
    6. Make up the final volume to 150 ml with sterilized distilled water
    7. Sterilize the solution by autoclaving as mentioned above
    8. Store the solution at 4 Â°C
    On the day of the experiment aliquot of 25 ml of the solution and add complete protease inhibitor just prior to use.
  3. 0.6 M sucrose solution
    1. Add 20.53 g of sucrose (M.W. = 342.29 g/mol) to 80 ml sterilized dH2O in a sterilized 250 ml bottle
    2. Dissolve the reagent completely
    3. Make up the final volume to 100 ml and sterilize by autoclaving as mentioned above
    4. Store at 4 Â°C
  4. 0.1 M sodium citrate buffer
    1. Add 2.58 g of sodium citrate (M.W. = 258.06 g/mol) to 80 ml sterilized dH2O in a sterilized 250 ml bottle
    2. Dissolve the reagent completely
    3. Adjust the pH to 5.0 using 1 M HCl or 1 M NaOH (as required)
    4. Add 10 ml of glycerol solution
    5. Make up the volume to 100 ml and sterilize by autoclaving as mentioned above
    6. Store at 4 Â°C
  5. Rat liver cytosol
    1. Euthanize a Sprague Dawley rat
    2. Perfuse the liver with 0.9% NaCl solution in situ via hepatic portal vein until fully blanched (Wen et al., 2012)
    3. Excise out the blanched liver and mince it completely with help of scissors
    4. Homogenize in 5 ml of 0.1 M sodium citrate buffer pH 5 containing 10% glycerol
    5. Centrifuge the obtained homogenate at 10,000 x g for 20 min
    6. Collect the resulting supernatant carefully and ultracentrifuge at 105,000 x g for 1 h
    7. Collect the obtained supernatant carefully and store in aliquots at -80 Â°C for use
    Note: Use the prepared as early as possible as enzymes gradually loose their activity upon storage.
  6. Bone marrow derived macrophages (BMDM)
    1. Euthanize the mouse
    2. Lay the mouse in a supine position and affix it by pinning the four legs through the mouse paw pads below the ankle joint
    3. Thoroughly spray the mouse abdomen and hind legs with 70% ethanol
    4. Make an incision at the top of each hind leg and pull the skin outwards exposing the muscle using sterile scissors and forceps
    5. Use scissors to remove maximum muscles and connective tissues from the bones. Carefully isolate the entire femurs and tibia without cutting the bone ends or breaking the bones
    6. Clean the bones from any attached tissues with help of tissue papers
    7. Transfer the bones with help of forceps in a Petri dish containing 70% ethanol (EtOH) for one minute followed by air dry for about 5 min inside cell culture hood
    8. Cut both ends of the isolated bones carefully so that the bones do not get shattered
      Notes:
      1.  Hold the femur or tibia with help of forceps. Place the scissors just above the joint to prevent the bone from shattering. Turn the bone and repeat the procedure.
      2. Do not let the cut ends of the bones touch anything as this leads to contamination of the marrow.
    9. Flush out the bone marrow into a 50 ml Falcon tube by inserting a 26-gauge needle attached to a 10 ml syringe filled with 1x PBS (containing Antibiotic solution) at the knee side of both types of bone. Pass the PBS through the bone until the colour of the bone turns white from reddish, indicating that most of the marrow has been expelled
    10. Discard the bone into an empty Petri dish
      Notes:
      1. Perform these steps as early as possible for each bone IMMEDIATELY following the cutting of the bone.
      2. DO NOT put a bone down between cutting and flushing.
    11. Centrifuge the collected cell suspension at 1,500 rpm for 10 min at 4 Â°C
    12. Wash the cell pellet again with 1x PBS
    13. Resuspend the cells in RPMI 1640, supplemented with 100 U/ml penicillin and 100 Âµg/ml streptomycin, 10% FCS, and 25 ng/ml GM-CSF and were incubated at 5% CO2, 95% humidity, at 37 Â°C
    14. On the 4th day, the media will be discarded and add new media containing 100 U/ml penicillin and 100 Âµg/ml streptomycin, 10% FCS, and 25 ng/ml GM-CSF
    15. On Day 6, BMDM cells will be obtained for usage
