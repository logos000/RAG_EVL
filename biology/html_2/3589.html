Auxin-mediated Protein Degradation in Caenorhabditis elegans

Authors: Michael A. Q. Martinez, David Q. Matus

Abstract

The auxin-inducible degron (AID) technology was recently adapted for use in the nematode Caenorhabditis elegans. Rapid degradation of C. elegans proteins tagged with an AID is mediated by a plant-specific F-box protein, transport inhibitor response 1 (TIR1), and occurs only in the presence of the phytohormone auxin. The first iteration of this technology elicited protein degradation in C. elegans through a naturally occurring form of auxin, indole-3-acetic acid (IAA). Here, we present a protocol that uses 1-naphthaleneacetic acid, potassium salt (K-NAA), an indole-free synthetic auxin analog. At equal concentration, K-NAA is as effective as IAA in standard nematode growth media (NGM). K-NAA is also effective in physiological buffer (M9), allowing for high-throughput experimentation. The main advantages of K-NAA are twofold: first, its photostability prevents light-induced compound degradation during storage and the production of toxic indole-derivatives during fluorescence microscopy of live cells; and second, its water solubility eliminates the need of using ethanol to dissolve the auxin compound, a solvent that may confound C. elegans lifespan and behavioral assays. In this protocol, we describe our method of degrading C. elegans proteins using K-NAA on solid and in liquid media, as well as our method of analyzing protein degradation.

Keywords: AID technology, Protein degradation, C. elegans, Synthetic auxin, K-NAA

Background

Conditional protein degradation through degrons is an emerging method for studying protein function in Caenorhabditis elegans (Nance and FrÃ¸kjÃ¦r-Jensen, 2019). Current degron methods include ZF1 (Armenti et al., 2014; Sallee et al., 2018), auxin-inducible degron (AID) (Zhang et al., 2015; Martinez et al., 2019), and GFP nanobody-mediated protein degradation (Wang et al., 2017). Originally, the auxin-based degron method was co-opted from plants and transplanted into nonplant systems such as yeast, chicken and mammalian cell culture (Nishimura et al., 2009). This method was recently optimized for use in C. elegans due to advances in CRISPR/Cas9 gene-editing technology (Zhang et al., 2015; Dickinson and Goldstein, 2016). In the presence of the plant hormone auxin, C. elegans proteins tagged with an AID are rapidly degraded by a heterologously expressed plant-specific F-box protein, transport inhibitor response 1 (TIR1). TIR1 functions as a substrate-recognition component of the CUL-1-based SCF E3 ubiquitin ligase complex consisting of SKR-1/2, CUL-1 and the RING component RBX-1 (Martinez et al., 2019). In as little as 30 min, C. elegans proteins exposed to auxin can be precisely degraded in a tissue- and cell-specific manner (Zhang et al., 2015; Martinez et al., 2019).

Either the natural auxin indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) or the synthetic auxin 1-naphthaleneacetic acid (NAA) can be used to conditionally deplete target proteins (Zhang et al., 2015; Martinez et al., 2019). However, NAA has the advantage over IAA partly due to its photostability (Yamakawa et al., 1979). Blue light, used to excite GFP, in combination with IAA has been shown to cause defects in mitosis and meiosis in yeast and mammalian oocytes, respectively (Papagiannakis et al., 2017; Camlin and Evans, 2019). Such effects likely occur through the photo-destruction of IAA to its toxic indole derivatives (Folkes and Wardman, 2001; Srivastava, 2002). Furthermore, the potassium salt of NAA (K-NAA) has the extra advantage of being completely water-soluble. This circumvents the need to expose C. elegans to low percentages of ethanol, used to dissolve IAA, or other potentially harmful solvents (Li et al., 2019). Additionally, when compared to 4 mM IAA, an equivalent concentration of K-NAA does not affect growth of C. elegans bacterial food Escherichia coli OP50 (Martinez et al., 2019). Also, 4 mM IAA results in significant embryonic lethality compared to K-NAA of the same concentration (Martinez et al., 2019). Potential applications of this protocol include high-throughput, high-resolution chemical genetic screens using a microfluidic device or a 96-well plate where worms are immersed in K-NAA. The protocol described here will elaborate on a simple procedure where C. elegans larvae are immersed in K-NAA diluted in M9 physiological buffer on a spot plate or exposed to K-NAA diluted in traditional nematode growth media (NGM). Finally, a step-by-step procedure to quantify the extent of protein degradation is presented.

### Procedure

  1. L1 stage synchronization by sodium hypochlorite treatment
    Note: Synchronization via egg lay is an alternative method that can be used (Gidalevitz et al., 2009).
    1. Prepare NGM plates (Recipe 7).
    2. Seed NGM plates with 200-300 Î¼l of overnight grown E. coli OP50 under sterile conditions. Allow the plates to dry at room temperature for approximately 48 h. Store at 4 Â°C until use. Warm plates at room temperature before use.
    3. Using a worm picker, pick the desired number of L4 stage CA1202 worms, or any AID-tagged strain of interest, onto NGM plates seeded with E. coli OP50. Allow the worms to grow until adulthood. Depending on the rate of usage, grow between 15-25 Â°C (Stiernagle, 2006).
    4. Recover gravid adults in a 15 ml centrifuge tube by washing plates with ddH2O.
    5. Add ddH2O to a total volume of 8 ml.
    6. Add 600 Î¼l of 5 M KOH.
    7. Add 1,200 Î¼l of 6.0% sodium hypochlorite solution.
    8. Place worms on a low speed tube rocker for 7-9 min.
    9. Pellet released eggs by centrifuging for 1 min at 400 x g at room temperature using a benchtop centrifuge. Quickly but carefully decant the supernatant.
      Note: Use a balancer containing 9.8 ml of ddH2O, if needed.
    10. Add 10 ml of ddH2O to the pellet. Gently mix the tube by inverting 3-4 times.
    11. Pellet released eggs by centrifuging for 2 min at 400 x g at room temperature using a benchtop centrifuge. Quickly but carefully decant the supernatant.
      Note: Use a balancer containing 10 ml of ddH2O, if needed.
    12. Add 10 ml of 1x M9 buffer to the pellet. Gently mix the tube by inverting 3-4 times.
    13. Repeat Step A11.
    14. Add 1x M9 buffer to the pellet to a final volume of 3 ml and incubate eggs at 20 Â°C on a low speed tube rocker.
    15. After an incubation time of 16 h, add the desired concentration of L1 worms to 2-3 NGM plates seeded with E. coli OP50.
    16. Allow worms to grow to the desired developmental stage using standard growth conditions (Stiernagle, 2006).

  2. Auxin treatment on solid media
    1. Prepare NGM plates containing 1 mM or 4 mM K-NAA (Recipe 8).
    2. Seed NGM plates containing K-NAA with 200-300 Î¼l of overnight grown E. coli OP50 under sterile conditions. Allow the plates to dry at room temperature for approximately 48 h. Store at 4 Â°C until use. Warm plates at room temperature before use.
    3. Once at the desired developmental stage, recover synchronized worms in 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tubes by washing plates with 500 Î¼l of 1x M9 buffer.
    4. Gently pellet worms by centrifuging for 2 min at 200 x g at room temperature using a microcentrifuge.
    5. Carefully remove as much of the supernatant as possible with a micropipette and discard it.
    6. Mix the remaining contents (i.e., worms and residual bacterial food) by gently tapping the bottom of the tube.
    7. Evenly dispense the contents onto 2-3 NGM plates containing K-NAA seeded with E. coli OP50 (100-200 worms per plate).
    8. Wait as little as 30 min to observe auxin-induced degradation of AID-tagged C. elegans proteins under a spinning disk confocal microscope (Figure 1). At this time, review the preparation of an agarose pad containing NaN3 (Recipe 14 and Procedure D).


      Figure 1. K-NAA-mediated degradation in C. elegans. Differential interference contrast (DIC) and corresponding green fluorescent protein (GFP) images of uterine anchor cells (ACs) (arrowheads) at the mid-L3 stage of C. elegans larval development. Scale bar, 5 Î¼m.

  3. Auxin treatment in liquid media
    1. Prepare 1 mM or 4 mM K-NAA in 1x M9 buffer (Recipe 10). Store at 4 Â°C until use. Warm the solution at room temperature before use.
    2. Prepare an in-house humidity incubation box (Figure 2).


      Figure 2. An in-house humidity incubation box. Wet paper towels are placed along the four bottom edges of an empty pipette box and dry paper towel is attached to the inner surface of the lid. The wet paper towels should create a pocket for a spot plate to fit in.

    3. Once at the desired developmental stage, recover synchronized worms (100-200 worms per well) on a spot plate by washing plates with 500 Î¼l of K-NAA in 1x M9 buffer.
      Note: Directly washing worms off plates transfers the bacterial food source, preventing short-term starvation (~2 h). Additional bacterial food can be re-suspended as needed. We recommend allowing the worms to thrash freely on a spot plate and not in a 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tube as it decreases degradation efficiency in our hands.
    4. Place the spot plate in the in-house humidity incubation box (Figure 2). Close the lid on the box.
    5. Wait as little as 30 min to observe auxin-induced degradation of AID-tagged C. elegans proteins under a spinning disk confocal microscope (Figure 1). At this time, review the preparation of an agarose pad containing NaN3 (Recipe 14 and Procedure D).

  4. Worm mounting and anesthetization
    1. After 30 min, gently break apart the two microscope slides interfacing with the agarose pad containing NaN3. Use the microscope slide with the agarose pad containing anesthetic.
      Note: We recommend using the agarose pad within 5 min to prevent desiccation.
    2. For auxin treatment on solid media, add 1.2 Î¼l of 1x M9 buffer to the center of the pad. Pick 30-50 worms and gently dab them on the M9. Use a dissecting microscope to ensure the worms have fully transferred off the worm picker.
    3. For auxin treatment in liquid media, remove 1.2-2 Î¼l with a micropipette from the center of the spot plate well and drop the worms onto the center of the pad.
    4. Gently place a microscope cover slip over the agar pad.
      Note: To avoid air bubbles underneath the cover slip, place the cover slip at a 45-degree angle and gently drop the cover slip over the agar pad. The cover slip should be applied gently to avoid vulval expulsion. We do not recommend pressing down on the cover slip once it has been released.

  5. Worm imaging
    1. Place the microscope slide on the stage and secure it with the stage clips.
    2. Move the 10x objective lens into position.
    3. Focus on the worm using the coarse focus knob.
    4. Finish focusing on the worm using the fine focus knob.
    5. Place a small drop of oil on the cover slip.
    6. Move the 100x objective lens into position.
    7. Slowly focus on the tissue or cell of interest using the fine focus knob.
    8. Collect 16-bit z-stack images using an EM-CCD camera attached to a spinning disk confocal microscope.
    9. Imaging parameters (e.g., exposure settings and laser power) should be documented and kept consistent.

### Data analysis

  1. Open ImageJ software (version: 2.0.0-rc-69/1.52p).
  2. Click Analyze > Set Measurements. Make sure that mean gray value is selected.
  3. Drag and drop TIFF file containing DIC z-stack image.
  4. Scroll through DIC z-stack image to find the cell of interest.
  5. Zoom in on the cell of interest.
  6. Use the freehand selection tool to outline the cell of interest (Figure 3).


    Figure 3. A screenshot image of an outlined AC revealed by DIC microscopy

  7. Click Analyze > Tools > ROI Manager.
  8. Click Add[t].
  9. Drag and drop TIFF file containing corresponding fluorescent protein z-stack image.
  10. Click the ROI (Figure 4).


    Figure 4. A screenshot image of an outlined AC in a transgenic worm expressing eft-3>AID::GFP

  1. Click Analyze > Measure.
  2. Drag freehand selection to a background region in the image.
  3. Click Analyze > Measure.
  4. Subtract the mean gray value of the background from the mean gray value of the cell of interest. This subtraction accounts for camera noise.
  5. Document the calculated value in a spreadsheet and repeat analysis for each imaged worm.
  6. Normalize the data by dividing the cellâs mean gray value upon auxin treatment by the cellâs mean gray value prior to auxin treatment.
  7. Perform a minimum of three independent experiments per treatment condition examining at least 20-30 worms per experiment.

### Notes

It has not been tested whether a higher concentration of K-NAA (> 4 mM) will inhibit E. coli OP50 growth, or whether it will lead to faster depletion of AID-tagged proteins in C. elegans (Martinez et al., 2019). By and large, degradation rates are similar in the presence of 1 mM and 4 mM auxin (Zhang et al., 2015; Martinez et al., 2019). Some endogenously tagged proteins seem to respond better to 4 mM auxin, such as the nuclear hormone receptor NHR-25 (Martinez et al., 2019). However, we currently believe that the rate-limiting factor in the AID system is the auxin receptor TIR1.

    1. Dissolve 280.55 g of KOH in 1 L of ddH2O
    2. Aliquot 200 ml into 500 ml storage bottles and store at room temperature
  2. 5 mg/ml cholesterol
    1. Dissolve 2.5 g of cholesterol in 500 ml of 100% ethanol
    2. Mix at 30 Â°C for 15-30 min to dissolve
    3. Aliquot 100 ml into 250 ml storage bottles
    4. Store at room temperature
  3. 1 M CaCl2 stock solution
    Dissolve 73.5 g of CaCl2 in 500 ml of ddH2O and autoclave for 30 min
    Store at room temperature
  4. 1 M MgSO4 stock solution
    Dissolve 60.2 g of MgSO4 in 500 ml of ddH2O and autoclave for 30 min
    Store at room temperature
  5. 1 M KPO4, pH 6.0 stock solution
    1. Dissolve 68.0 g of KH2PO4 in 500 ml of ddH2O
    2. Dissolve 52.3 g of K2HPO4 in 300 ml of ddH2O
    3. While measuring the pH of 1 M KH2PO4, add and stir 1 M K2HPO4 slowly until the pH rises from 4.0 to 6.0
    4. Aliquot and autoclave for 30 min
    5. Store at room temperature
  6. 250 mM K-NAA stock solution
    1. Dissolve 2.243 g of K-NAA in 40 ml of ddH2O in a 50 ml centrifuge tube and vortex at room temperature to fully dissolve the compound
    2. Clarify solution through a sterile syringe filter connected to a 30 ml syringe into a new 50 ml centrifuge tube
    3. Store at 4 Â°C for up to six months
  7. NGM plates
    1. Add 6 g of NaCl, 5 g of peptone, 34 g of bacteriological agar, and a magnetic stirring bar to a 4 L Erlenmeyer flask
    2. Add 1,944 ml of ddH2O
    3. Autoclave for 30 min
    4. Let the NGM cool to 55-60 Â°C.
    5. Add 2 ml of 5 mg/ml cholesterol, 2 ml of 1 M CaCl2, 2 ml of 1 M MgSO4, and 50 ml of 1 M KPO4, pH 6.0
    6. Thoroughly mix the medium in the flask on a stir plate
    7. Dispense 8 ml per 60 x 15 mm Petri dish and allow them to solidify for 48 h
  8. NGM plates containing K-NAA
    1. Add K-NAA to a desired final concentration immediately prior to dispensing the autoclaved NGM
    2. For 1 mM K-NAA, add 32 Î¼l of 250 mM K-NAA. For 4 mM K-NAA, add 128 Î¼l of 250 mM K-NAA
    3. Plates can be stored at 4 Â°C for up to a month
  9. 1x M9 buffer
    1. Add 6 g of Na2HPO4, 3 g of KH2PO4, and 5 g of NaCl to a 1 L storage bottle
    2. Add 1 L of ddH2O
    3. Aliquot 200 ml into 500 ml storage bottles and autoclave for 30 min
    4. Add 200 Î¼l of 1 M MgSO4 to each bottle
    5. Store at room temperature
  10. K-NAA in 1x M9 buffer
    1. Dilute K-NAA in 1x M9 buffer to a desired final concentration and final volume of 10 ml
    2. For 1 mM K-NAA, dilute 40 Î¼l of 250 mM K-NAA in 960 Î¼l of 1x M9 buffer. For 4 mM K-NAA, dilute 160 Î¼l of 250 mM K-NAA in 840 Î¼l of 1x M9 buffer
    3. Solution can be stored at 4 Â°C for up to a month
  11. 5% agarose
    1. Add 5 g of melting agarose and a magnetic stirring bar to a 250 ml storage bottle
    2. Add 100 ml of ddH2O
    3. Microwave until agarose is completely dissolved
    4. Keep the agarose on a stir plate and aliquot 5 ml into 9 ml glass culture tubes
    5. Allow it to solidify
    6. Store at room temperature
  12. 1 M NaN3 stock solution
    1. Dissolve 0.65 g of NaN3 in 10 ml of ddH2O
    2. Sterilize solution and aliquot into 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tubes
    3. Store at room temperature for up to 2-3 months
  13. Agarose containing NaN3
    1. Gently microwave a 5 ml aliquot of 5% agarose for 7 s
    2. Using a Bunsen burner, carefully and thoroughly melt the agarose
    3. Keep it on a dry bath incubator at 65 Â°C to prevent it from solidifying
    4. Add 35 Î¼l of 1 M NaN3 to 5 ml of melted 5% agarose for a final concentration of 7 mM
    5. Resuspend to mix
    6. Prepare fresh every 24 h or prior to each imaging session
  14. Agarose pad containing NaN3
    1. Carefully snap off the tip from a 2 ml glass pipette
    2. Fit a rubber pipette bulb on the top of the glass pipette
    3. Using the rubber pipette bulb attached to a glass pipette, aspirate and drop a small volume of agarose containing NaN3 onto a microscope slide. Avoid bubbles
    4. Quickly and perpendicularly place a second microscope slide over the slide containing the drop of agarose with anesthetic. Allow the agarose to solidify
      Note: We recommend vertically flanking the base microscope slide with taped microscope slides of the same dimension in parallel on a flat level surface. Prepare fresh every 20 min.
